COVID-19 Myths

PUBLISHED: January 9, 2022

LAST UPDATED: January 9, 2022

  • ► Myths
  • ► Questions

Below are some common myths or questions regarding the COVID-19 disease or coronavirus. Being able to differentiate between real and false and information is necessary to not fall for hoaxes that can potentially harm you or other people.

1. Myth: Ivermectin cures or prevents COVID-19. False

Ivermectin, a drug that controls parasites, has been reported to be effective against COVID-19 multiple times. However, these rumors have not yet been confirmed as the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has not authorized the use of Ivermectin against COVID-19. Nonapproved usage of this drug may be harmful and can lead to hospitalization or death.[1]

2. Myth: Natural immunity is more effective than COVID-19 vaccines. False

Getting vaccinated for COVID-19 is safer, as the vaccines are more reliable at protecting the body from the virus. Your body's immune response with vaccines will also be stronger and more consistent compared to natural immunity, which would vary between people.[2]

3. Myth: COVID-19 vaccines have microchips in them. False

Vaccines contain antibodies to help prevent getting COVID-19. They do not have microchips in their ingredients.[2]

4. Myth: COVID-19 cannot spread in hot climates. False

COVID-19 can spread in any climate, cold or not.[3] This sentence may have been confused with the fact that COVID-19 tends to live longer in colder environments and last shorter in hotter climates.[4] While this is true, it doesn't mean that the virus cannot spread during warm weathers.

5. Myth: Swallowing alcohol/bleach or rubbing them on your body can protect you against COVID-19. False

These substances are toxic and highly dangerous and must not be consumed. They are only meant to be used to clean surfaces, except for hand sanitizers, but those are only applied on hands.[2]

6. Question: Can children be infected by COVID-19? Yes

Children can be infected with COVID-19, and some have also died from it. That's why children still need to stay indoors. If vaccines for children are available in your area, it's highly encouraged to have them vaccinated.[2]

7. Question: Are digital thermometers effective at identifying COVID-19? Yes

...but only for symptomatic patients. People with COVID-19 that don't show any signs of the disease are asymptomatic, and their symptoms can't easily be traced by digital thermometers and such.[3]

8. Question: Should I always wash my hands and wear masks? Yes

Absolutely. Wearing masks and washing hands prevent the spread of COVID-19, as well as maintaining social distancing (preferably 6 feet away from sick people), and avoiding crowds of people. This way, you prevent yourself and others from getting COVID-19.[5]
