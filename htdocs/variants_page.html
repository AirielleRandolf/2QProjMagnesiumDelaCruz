COVID-19 Variants

PUBLISHED: January 9, 2022

LAST UPDATED: January 9, 2022

What are Variants?

All viruses, including SARS-CoV-2, change over time via mutation -- a change in the virus' genetic code. Sometimes, when these mutations are significant enough, a new variant of the virus emerges. Lineages, a group of viruses with the same common ancestor, can also form. [1] Changes from these mutations can increase the virus' lifespan, transmissibility, disease severity, and so on.[2]

There are three major categories that a variant can be in:[3]

  1. Variant under Monitoring - A variant that is suspected to affect the virus' characteristics and is under assessment for further evidence.[2]

  2. Variant of Interest - A variant that is known or predicted to affect the virus' characteristics with increasing relative prevalence.[2]

  3. Variant of Concern - A variant of interest that has become significant for the global public health.[2]

  4. Variant of High Consequence - A variant that is incredibly severe and shows evidence of reduced vaccine effectiveness.[3]



This page will look at the current variants of interest (VOIs) and variants of concern (VOCs) inside the cards below. There are currently no variants of high consequence (VOHCs).

Variants of Concern (WHO)

Alpha

Alpha Variant

The Alpha Variant, also known as B.1.1.7 (its lineage), was first detected in the United Kingdom in November/early December of 2020, but was traced to have existed since September 2020.[4] This variant is also known as 20I (V1) and GRY.[2]



Transmissibility

The Alpha variant, according to this study by Davies et al. (2021), is estimated to be around 43% to 90% more transmissible than any of the SARS-Cov-2 variants prior.[5] A separate study by Volz et al. (2021) shows that the Alpha variant "has a substantial transmission advantage over other lineages, with a 50% to 100% higher reproduction number."[6]

The most significant mutation from the Alpha variant is an N501Y mutation on the spike protein, which the virus uses to bind to human ACE2 receptors, theoretically making it more infectious.[7]



Virulence / Lethality

Some studies, like this one by Challen et al. (2021), show a higher mortality rate for B.1.1.7.[8] Other studies like this ecological study by Graham et al. (2021) shows no significant difference in virulence.[9]



Spread throughout the World

The Alpha variant started its spread in the United Kingdom, and has since been found in 175 countries (as of Dec-28-2021).[10] As of 29 December 2021, over 1.1 million sequences in the B.1.1.7 lineage have been detected worldwide.[11]

Beta

Beta Variant

The Beta Variant, also known as B.1.351 (its lineage), first appeared in the Eastern Cape of South Africa in December of 2020.[12] Spatiotemporal phylogeographic analysis shows that the virus may have existed since early July or August 2020.[13]



Disease severity and Transmissibility

The Beta variant seems to increase transmission and severity among the youth and also seems to produce serious illnesses more frequently than previous variants. Former Minister of Health Zweli Mkhize stated of evidence showing that the second wave of Covid-19 cases in South Africa was driven by the Beta variant.[14]



Vaccine efficacy

The Beta variant is less susceptible to vaccines, especially spike protein-dependent vaccines, due to its E484K mutation that lets it escape from neutralizing antibodies.[15]

×

Gamma

Gamma Variant

The Gamma Variant, also known as P.1, came from Amazones, Brazil and was first detected in Japan in January 2021.[16]



Viral structure

The key mutations of the Gamma Variant are N501Y (increase in transmission), K417T (increase in transmission), E484K (reduced vaccine efficacy). All of these mutations are also present in the Beta Variant, making the Gamma Variant very similar to the former variant in terms of structure.[17]



Spread throughout the world

As of January 9, 2021, 63 countries have the Gamma variant with over 100,000 cases worldwide.[18]

×

Delta

Delta Variant

The Delta Variant, also known as B.1.617.2, was first detected in India in October 2020 and has, as of late 2021, become the leading variant of the world.[19]



Transmissibility

According to reports from UK scientists, the Delta variant is roughly 40% to 60% more transmissible than the Alpha variant from UK. This would mean that the Delta variant would be more than 200% more transmissible than the original SARS-CoV-2 strain.[20] A non-peer reviewed study by Li et al. (2021) showed that the viral load of Delta (the amount of viruses in the body) was approximately 1000 times more than initial variants, contributing to its transmissibility.[21]



Virulence

According to a paper posted by researchers at National Centre for Infectious Diseases in Singapore, victims of the Delta Variant are more likely to develop oxygen-requiring illnesses (e.g., pneumonia) and death than victims of the Alpha or earlier variants.[22] A weekly report of the WHO also stated that the Delta variant gave an "increased risk of hospitalization" for its victims.[23]



Spread throughout the world

As of January 9, 2021, there are 178 countries infected by the Delta variant, with over 3 million cases worldwide.[24]

×

Omicron

Omicron Variant

The Omicron Variant, also known as the B.1.1.529, was first detected from South Africa in 24th of November, 2021.[25] It was detected in a sample from November 9 of that year.



Transmissibility

As of November 26, 2021, it's not clear whether or not the Omicron variant is more contagious than its previous counterparts.[26]



Severity

It's also not yet clear whether or not the Omicron variant causes more severe diseases than the Delta variant. There have been an increasing rate of hospitalization in South Africa, but whether or not this is due to the Omicron variant is still unclear as of November 2021.[26] A study by Ferguson et al. (2021) even showed a decreased risk of hospitalization from the Omicron variant when compared to the Delta variant.[27] Still, it is imperative for people to follow the WHO's guidelines to avoid contracting this new variant of COVID-19.

×

Variants of Interest (WHO)

Lambda

Lambda Variant

The Lambda variant, also known as C.37, was first detected in Peru in August of 2020.[28] The variant was designated to "Variant of Interests" in June 14, 2021.[2]



Spread throughout the world

As of January 9, 2022, there are over 9,000 cases of the Lambda variant in 40+ countries throughout the world.[29]

×

Mu

Mu Variant

The Mu Variant, also known as B.1.621, was first detected in Colombia in January 2021.[2]



Spread throughout the world

As of January 9, 2021, the Mu variant has over 14,000 cases worldwide in 50+ countries.[30]
